01 · Platform Configuration
Simply.Coach — Step by Step
Simply.Coach is your coaching backend: session notes, client portal, goal tracking, and invoicing in one place. Set it up once and it runs. These steps take about two hours total.
Step 1 · Account and Branding
Make It Feel Like Yours
Upload your logo — the Greer Collective wordmark. Settings → Branding.
Set your brand colors — Primary: #2C1A2E (Plum), Accent: #B8922A (Gold).
Write your coach bio — this appears in the client portal. Use the short version from your OnePager: 3–4 sentences, active voice, no em dashes.
Connect your Zoom account — Settings → Integrations → Zoom. Every session will auto-generate a unique Zoom link.
Connect your Google Calendar — prevents double-booking and syncs session times.
Step 2 · Session Types
Create One Session Type Per Package
Clarity Call — 90 minutes, $250–$350. Single session. Notes template: see below.
Intensive Session — 60 minutes, bi-weekly. Used for all 6 sessions in the Intensive package.
Deep Dive Session — 60 minutes, bi-weekly. Used for all 12 sessions in the Deep Dive package.
Group Cohort Session — 90 minutes, monthly. Set capacity to 8 max.
Discovery Call — 45 minutes, complimentary. This is your top-of-funnel session. Mark it as free.
Do not create hourly session types. Package billing only — clients pay for the engagement, not the hour.

Step 4 · Goal Tracking
Set Goals at Session 1 — Track Through Session 6
In Simply.Coach, create 2–3 goals per client in the first session. These are the client's words, not coaching-speak.
Use the progress indicator to update goal status at each session — "not yet started / in progress / achieved / sustained."
At the mid-point (Intensive: Session 3–4 / Deep Dive: Month 3), run a formal goal review and note which goals have shifted or been replaced.
At the final session, document the achievement status of every goal. This becomes part of your case study and testimonial request.

Step 6 · ICF Hours Log
Track Hours from Day One — Every Session Counts
Simply.Coach logs session duration automatically. Export monthly to confirm your hours count.
For ICF: you need 100 coaching hours for ACC (up to 25 can be pro bono), and 10 hours of mentor coaching. Track these as separate categories.
Keep a separate simple spreadsheet: Client | Date | Duration | Paid or Pro Bono | Package. This is the document you submit with your ACC application.
Do not wait until you apply to reconstruct your hours. Log them in real time starting with your first session.

03 · Client Onboarding
What Happens Before Session 1
First impressions in coaching are not just about the first session — they are about everything that happens before it. A clean, warm, professional onboarding process signals the quality of what follows.
After Discovery Call — Client Says Yes
Send Within 24 Hours
Coaching agreement — covers confidentiality, session structure, cancellation policy, and payment terms. Must be signed before Session 1. Keep it to 1–2 pages.
Invoice or payment link — collect the first payment (or full package payment) before the first session. Simply.Coach handles invoicing and stripe payments.
Welcome email — warm, brief. Confirm the first session date, send the Calendly link for remaining sessions, and include one sentence about what to expect.
Required fields in your coaching agreement: client name, engagement type, session count, investment, payment schedule, confidentiality clause, cancellation policy (24-hour notice minimum), scope of coaching vs. therapy disclaimer.
Before Session 1 — Client Action
Send the CliftonStrengths Access Code (Intensive + Deep Dive)
Purchase the code from Gallup ($19.99 Top 5 / $49.99 Full 34) and send it with clear instructions: complete the assessment before Session 1, bring the report PDF.
Send the pre-session intake form (via Simply.Coach): 4–5 questions about their goals, leadership context, and what success looks like at the end of the engagement.
Add them to your Simply.Coach client portal — they will be able to see session notes you mark as shared, their goals, and upcoming sessions.

What the Intake Form Should Ask
1. What brings you to coaching at this point in your career?
2. What would feel different about your leadership if this engagement is successful?
3. What is one thing you want me to know about how you work and learn best?
4. Is there anything you are hoping coaching will not ask of you? (This is an optional question — but the answers are often the most useful.)
5. What is the most pressing situation in your work life right now?
04 · Coaching Tips
What Separates Good Coaching from Great Coaching
These are not rules — they are things that actually matter in the room. They come from ICF competencies, from what top coaches consistently name, and from what you already do well that is worth making more intentional.
Tip 01
Silence is your most powerful tool.
When a client answers a question, most coaches nod and ask the next one. The best coaches pause. Let the silence breathe. The client's second and third thoughts — what surfaces after the rehearsed answer — are usually where the real work lives.
"Take a moment with that." Then stop talking. Count to ten internally if you have to.
Tip 02
Ask one question at a time. One.
When coaches are nervous or excited, they ask multiple questions in one breath. The client answers the easiest one and the harder questions evaporate. Choose the question that goes deepest and ask only that one. Let them answer it fully.
Not: "What happened and how did that feel and what are you going to do about it?" Just: "What happened?"
Tip 03
Reflect back in their words, not yours.
When you paraphrase in your own vocabulary, you introduce your frame. When you reflect back the exact words they used — especially the emotionally loaded ones — they hear themselves differently. You do not need to add anything. Just hand the words back.
"You said it felt like disappearing. Say more about that."
Tip 04
Don't advise when you can ask.
When you know the answer — and you often will — the instinct is to offer it. Resist. Ask the question that leads them to it instead. They will own it differently. "What do you think you already know?" is more powerful than the most elegant piece of advice you could give.
"What would you tell a colleague in this exact situation?" Then listen while they give themselves the advice.
Tip 05
Notice what is not being said.
The shape of the conversation often matters as much as the content. Who is absent from a story that should be there? What emotion is in the room that they have not named? What question are they avoiding by giving you a lot of words? Name what you notice gently, without certainty.
"I notice we have talked for 20 minutes about this situation and you have not mentioned how you feel about it. What is true there?"
Tip 06
Coach the person, not the problem.
Clients will bring you problems. Your job is not to solve the problem — it is to develop the person who is navigating the problem. The tactical question and the identity question are always both available. The identity question is almost always the more useful one.
Not: "Here's how to handle that performance situation." But: "What does this situation reveal about what you believe your job is?"
Tip 07
Challenge directly. Care first.
You are not doing your client a service by staying in comfortable territory. When you see a pattern, a contradiction, or a story that is keeping them stuck — name it. Do it with care and directness together. This is the heart of Radical Candor and it applies to your own coaching practice as much as you teach it to clients.
"I want to push back gently on something. You have described that person as the obstacle in three different sessions now. What if you are the variable you have not yet examined?"
Tip 08
Start and end on time. Always.
Time boundaries are a form of respect. A coach who lets sessions run over is not being generous — they are being unclear about their own structure. End with intention: summarize what the client named, confirm their action, and close warmly. The last five minutes are as important as the first five.
"We have about five minutes. I want to make sure you are leaving with what you need. What is the one thing you are taking?"
Tip 09
Take notes — but stay present.
Notes are for you, not the session. If you are writing so much that you are losing eye contact and presence, you are in reporter mode, not coaching mode. Jot keywords and phrases. Write the full notes immediately after. Your presence in the room is the most important thing you offer.
Tip 10
Your own self-management is a coaching tool.
What you feel in the session is often a mirror of what the client feels. Boredom may signal avoidance. Urgency may signal their anxiety landing in you. When you notice an internal state in session, ask: what is this in response to? Your body is giving you information about the client. Use it.
"I am noticing something in myself right now — a kind of urgency. I wonder if that is connected to something you are carrying into this session. What is true for you right now?"
The Two Questions That Work in Almost Every Session
"What do you already know?" — When a client is stuck, they usually already have the answer and are afraid to claim it. This question gives them permission to know what they know.

"What would you do if you let yourself do what you want to do?" — The word "let" carries all the weight here. Most leadership blocks are not about capability — they are about permission.
05 · Useful Scripts
Language for Situations That Come Up Often
These are not scripts to memorize — they are starting points you can adapt to your voice and the specific moment.
When a Client Wants Advice
Redirect Without Dismissing
"I hear you asking me what I would do. I will tell you — but first I want to know what you already think. What is your instinct here, if you set the fear aside?"
When a Client Arrives in Crisis Mode
Slow the Room Down First
"I can feel there is a lot happening for you right now. Before we go anywhere, I just want to check in: what do you most need from this session today? We can adjust the agenda completely."
When a Client Is Stuck in a Story
Interrupt With Care
"I want to pause you there — not because what you are saying is not important, but because something you said three sentences ago is still sitting with me. You said [PHRASE]. Can we go back to that?"
When You Want to Name What You Notice
Make It an Observation, Not a Diagnosis
"I want to name something I am noticing, and I hold this lightly — you can correct me. I have observed that every time [X topic] comes up, your energy shifts. What is true about that for you?"
Asking for a Testimonial
Session 4–5, Not at the End
"I want to ask you something — and please feel completely free to say no. I am building the written record of this work and the voices of people I have coached matter a great deal. Would you be willing to write a few sentences about what this experience has been like for you? What you share, what you want to keep private — entirely up to you."
Pitching a Beta Corporate Engagement
From the Pricing Strategy Guide
"I am completing an advanced ICF executive certification and am offering a heavily discounted 3-month strategic leadership package to a small number of high-caliber leaders — $2,500 instead of my post-credential rate of $6,000. I am looking for someone who is navigating a specific leadership challenge or transition and wants an objective sounding board to accelerate their thinking. Would you be open to a 45-minute conversation to see if it is a fit?"
