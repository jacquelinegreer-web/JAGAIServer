The books and articles that shaped how you built teams — from your first management roles at Urban Teachers through your ED and CPO work. These are the readings you assigned at retreats and lived out in practice.

Books
Retreat Reading · 2013
The Five Dysfunctions of a Team
Patrick Lencioni
Assigned at the 2013 and 2014 UTC site retreats. The pyramid model — trust, conflict, commitment, accountability, results — is already in your coaching practice. The Five Behaviors certification (Wiley) is the next-level credentialing path.
In Models Reference In Learning Plan
Retreat Reading · 2013 + 2014
Eight Behaviors for Smarter Teams
Roger Schwarz
Assigned twice across retreats. The Mutual Learning mindset — transparency, curiosity, informed choice — is a practical complement to Five Dysfunctions. Eight named behaviors give team coaching clients something concrete to build toward.
Add to Models Reference
Core Text
The Advantage
Patrick Lencioni
Organizational health as the ultimate competitive advantage. Makes the case that clarity, overcommunication, and reinforcement are the real drivers of performance — not strategy. Complements your Five Dysfunctions application with an organizational systems lens.

An Everyone Culture: Becoming a Deliberately Developmental Organization
Robert Kegan + Lisa Laskow Lahey
From the authors of Immunity to Change — extends that individual model to the organization. DDOs embed growth into everyday work rather than treating it as a separate initiative. Relevant for executive clients building development cultures.

Articles
01
Management Time: Who's Got the Monkey?
William Oncken Jr. + Donald Wass · HBR · Nov–Dec 1999
Assigned at 2013 UTC retreat. The central insight: managers unwittingly take on problems that belong to their direct reports. The "monkey" image is memorable and practically actionable. Use in manager-level coaching when the client is chronically overloaded.
02
On the Folly of Rewarding A, While Hoping for B
Steven Kerr · Academy of Management Executive · 1995
Classic systems article: organizations consistently reward behaviors they claim not to value. Assign to executive clients who say the culture is one thing and discover the incentives say something else. Powerful in combination with accountability conversations.
03
Combating the Culture of Can't
Dan Weisberg · TNTP · Education Sector
On how "it can't be done" becomes institutional default when leaders stop questioning inherited constraints. From your retreat pack, and directly relevant to coaching leaders who are inside systems that say no faster than they say yes. Useful in M3–M4 of Deep Dive work.

You built and changed organizations — Urban Teachers, the Black Educator Initiative, the national expansion work. These are the frameworks that name what you already know about why change fails and how to make it stick.

Books
Retreat Reading · 2013
The Inconvenient Truth About Change Management
Scott Keller + Carolyn Aiken · McKinsey Quarterly · 2008
From your 2013 retreat pack. The finding: 70% of change programs fail, and the reasons are predictable and largely irrational. Four counterintuitive insights about how employees actually respond to change. Use with executive clients managing organizational transitions.
Add to Learning Plan
Retreat Reading · 2015
Six Simple Rules: How to Manage Complexity Without Getting Complicated
Yves Morieux + Peter Tollman · BCG / Harvard Business Review Press
Smart Simplicity: instead of adding structure and process to manage complexity, give people the autonomy, resources, and judgment to handle it themselves. The six rules reframe how organizations design work. Relevant in executive coaching on organizational design and systems simplification.
Add to Learning Plan
Suggested
Leading Change
John P. Kotter
The canonical 8-step change model — referenced in McKinsey's "Inconvenient Truth" as the baseline that only 30% of organizations achieve. Foundational context for any change leadership coaching. Most of your clients will be somewhere in Kotter's model whether they know it or not.
Suggested
Suggested
Switch: How to Change Things When Change Is Hard
Chip Heath + Dan Heath
The Rider/Elephant/Path model — rational mind (Rider), emotional mind (Elephant), and environment (Path) must all be aligned for change to happen. Accessible and practically applicable. Use in manager-level coaching on behavior change and team adoption of new practices.

The Tyranny of Success
Michael Tushman · Fall 2014 Retreat Reading
On how past success becomes the enemy of future adaptation. Organizations and leaders get trapped by what made them good — and resist the exploration that would keep them relevant. Assign to executive clients who sense they need to change but do not know why they keep defaulting to old patterns.

Tony Schwartz + Catherine McCarthy · HBR · October 2007
Assigned at the 2015 retreat. The core insight: time is finite and fixed — energy is renewable if managed intentionally. Four energy dimensions: physical, emotional, mental, spiritual. Core Coaching Texts
ICF Foundation
Coaching for Performance
Sir John Whitmore
The book that popularized the GROW model. The philosophy: coaching unlocks a person's potential to maximize their own performance rather than teaching them. ICF coaches cite this text more than almost any other. Read it before ACC credential submission.
ICF Foundation Text
ICF Foundation
Immunity to Change
Robert Kegan + Lisa Laskow Lahey
Already in your models reference. The four-column map for understanding why committed, capable people do not change even when they want to. Facilitator certification through Minds at Work is the practitioner path. This text alone will make your Deep Dive work significantly more powerful.
In Models Reference
ICF Canon
Co-Active Coaching
Henry Kimsey-House, Karen Kimsey-House, Phillip Sandahl, Laura Whitworth
The foundational text of the CTI coaching model — co-active means both coach and client are active in the relationship. Deep treatment of presence, listening levels, intuition, and action. The most widely referenced coaching methodology text after GROW. Essential reading before PCC pursuit.
