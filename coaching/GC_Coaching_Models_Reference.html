Frameworks · Application Notes · Key Questions · When to Use Each
Framework 01
The GROW Model
Created by Graham Alexander, John Whitmore, and Alan Fine · Widely adopted in ICF-aligned coaching since the 1980s
GROW is the most widely used coaching framework in the world. It gives a coaching conversation a clear arc without becoming a script. Learn it first — everything else builds on it.
G
Goal
What does the client want from this session and from their work overall?
R
Reality
What is actually happening now? What has been tried?
O
Options
What are the possible paths forward? Generate, don't evaluate yet.
W
Will / Way Forward
What will they commit to? What is the specific next action?
When to Use GROW
When a client arrives with a clear presenting challenge or decision
For manager-level clients working through a specific situation
When you want a reliable structure in early sessions before you have the client's full context
As a check-in structure at the start of sessions (mini-GROW)
When GROW Is Not Enough
When the real issue is identity, not a problem to solve — GROW keeps clients in solution mode
When the "goal" itself needs examination — GROW assumes the goal is known
When psychological safety needs to be built first (use CLEAR instead)
When you are working with persistent patterns or unconscious blocks (use Immunity to Change)
Key Questions — By Stage
StageQuestionWhat It Does
Goal"What do you want to leave this session with?"Sets direction without closing off discovery
Goal"What would make this session feel like time well spent?"Client-owned outcome — not what you think they need
Reality"What is actually happening right now?"Separates the situation from the interpretation of it
Reality"What have you already tried?"Honors their effort and prevents redundant suggestions
Reality"On a scale of 1–10, how satisfied are you with where things are?"Anchors the gap. The follow-up is: "What would make it a 7?"
Options"What are all the possibilities — including the ones that feel too big or too small?"Expands thinking before evaluating
Options"If you knew it would work, what would you do?"Removes fear from the options conversation
Will"What specifically will you do, and by when?"Converts insight to commitment
Will"What might get in the way, and what will you do if it does?"Anticipates obstacles without catastrophizing
In Practice — What GROW Looks Like in a Real Session
A manager arrives and says: "I need to talk to a direct report about their attitude, but every time I try, it goes badly." You spend about 5 minutes in Goal (what do you want from this conversation and from today's session?), 10 minutes in Reality (what specifically happens? what does "badly" mean?), 10 minutes in Options (what approaches have you considered?), and 5 minutes in Will (what will you do before we meet again?). The conversation is not linear — you loop back. That is fine. GROW is a map, not a script.
Framework 02
The CLEAR Model
Developed by Peter Hawkins · Published in Coaching, Mentoring and Organizational Consultancy (2006)
CLEAR is built for contexts where psychological safety must come before problem-solving. It opens with an explicit contract and closes with a review — making it stronger than GROW for executive clients, complex organizational dynamics, and sensitive conversations.
C
Contract
What do we agree to work on? What does success look like today?
L
Listen
Deep listening. What is really being said beneath the presenting topic?
E
Explore
Expand the territory. What else is true? What assumptions are in play?
A
Action
What will the client do? Specific, chosen freely, owned completely.
R
Review
What happened in this session? What landed? What was the shift?
Why CLEAR Works for Executive Clients
The Contract phase names the power dynamic and makes it explicit — executives are used to setting terms, so giving them that space signals respect
The Listen phase is active and named — it legitimizes slowing down before solving, which is where most senior leaders struggle most
The Review phase closes the loop — clients leave knowing what happened, not just what they will do
Differences from GROW
GROW assumes a goal exists. CLEAR surfaces the right question before working on it.
CLEAR has an explicit listening phase — GROW assumes listening happens throughout
CLEAR closes with review — what happened in this session? Not just what will you do next.
CLEAR is better suited to identity and systemic work. GROW is better for discrete problems.
Key Questions — By Stage
  • Contract: "Before we start, what do you most want to walk away with today? And is there anything you want me to know about how this session should feel?"
  • Listen: "I want to make sure I am hearing you fully. What is underneath what you just described? What is the thing beneath the thing?"
  • Explore: "What else is true here that you haven't said yet?"
  • Explore: "If you were wrong about one thing in this situation, what might that be?"
  • Action: "Given everything we've surfaced, what feels most alive as a next step?"
  • Review: "Looking back at this session — what was the most significant moment for you?"
  • Review: "What shifted, if anything, in how you see this situation?"
When to Use CLEAR Instead of GROW
When a client arrives unsettled, emotionally activated, or uncertain about what the real issue is. When a client is a senior executive who tends to jump to solutions. When you are working on identity, not just behavior. When there is significant organizational complexity underneath a presenting problem. In your Deep Dive work, CLEAR is often the better session architecture for months 2, 4, and 6.
Framework 03
The OSKAR Model
Developed by Mark McKergow and Paul Z. Jackson · Solutions Focus approach · Published in The Solutions Focus (2002)
OSKAR is a solutions-focused coaching model. Unlike GROW or CLEAR — which move through problem analysis — OSKAR starts from the future state and works backward. Use it when clients are stuck in problem narratives and need to find what is already working.
O
Outcome
What is the desired outcome? What will be different when it is achieved?
S
Scaling
On a scale of 1–10, where are you now? What would one step up look like?
K
Know-how
What is already working? What resources and strengths are already present?
A
Affirm + Action
Acknowledge strengths, then choose one small specific action.
R
Review
What happened? What worked? What would you do more of?
When OSKAR Outperforms GROW
When a client is demoralized, stuck in what is wrong, or cannot see a path forward
When a client's "problem analysis" is circular and not producing new insight
For clients who have been through a failure or setback — OSKAR starts from where they want to go, not where they came from
When you want to build momentum with a specific, achievable action rather than a large commitment
The Philosophy Behind OSKAR
Solutions Focus principle: what works is what matters. You do not need to fully understand the problem to solve it.
Scaling questions are not about evaluation — they are about movement. The question is not "what score do you deserve?" It is "what would a 7 feel like?"
Know-how is one of the most underused coaching moves. Finding what is already working often unlocks more than analyzing what is not.
Key Questions
  • "Imagine you wake up six months from now and this is fully resolved. What is different? What do you notice first?"
  • "On a scale of 1–10, where are you on this right now? What is already at work that has gotten you to [that number] rather than zero?"
  • "What is one thing that is already working — even a little — that we could do more of?"
  • "What would one step up on that scale look like — not ten steps, just one?"
  • "Looking back at our last session: what worked? What would you do more of next time?"
Framework 04
Radical Candor
Kim Scott · Radical Candor: Be a Kick-Ass Boss Without Losing Your Humanity (2017) · radicalcandor.com
Radical Candor is a framework for honest leadership communication built on two axes: how much you personally care for someone, and how directly you challenge them. The goal is to do both at once. Most leaders default to one or the other — and usually at the wrong time.
The Four Quadrants
High Care · Low Challenge
Ruinous Empathy
The most common failure mode. You care about the person so much that you soften feedback to the point of uselessness. They do not get what they need to grow. You feel kind; they feel confused or blindsided later.
High Care · High Challenge
Radical Candor
You care deeply about the person and say the hard thing directly. The feedback lands because the relationship can hold it. This is what your best manager was doing when they were most useful to you.
Low Care · Low Challenge
Manipulative Insincerity
Saying what is convenient without meaning it. Organizational politics at its worst. Praise that is not earned, feedback that is not honest, agreements that are not meant.
Low Care · High Challenge
Obnoxious Aggression
Direct feedback delivered without care. Feels like an attack. Might be technically accurate — but it bruises trust and closes down the relationship. Sometimes confused with strength; it is not.
Coaching Application — Manager Level
Most manager clients live in Ruinous Empathy. They delay hard conversations, soften feedback until it is meaningless, and then feel resentful when people do not change.
Use the quadrant as a diagnostic in session: "Where are you on this specific conversation?"
Role-play a real conversation from Ruinous Empathy and then from Radical Candor — the contrast is instructive
The coaching question is not "why are you in Ruinous Empathy?" It is "what would it take to care enough to say the true thing?"
Coaching Application — Director and Executive Level
At director level, Obnoxious Aggression is often the failure mode — directness without the relational investment
Senior leaders often do not know what quadrant they are perceived to be in. 360 data paired with this framework is powerful.
The executive question: "What are you not saying to your board / your team / your skip-level that they need to hear?" That is almost always a Ruinous Empathy pattern at the top.
Frame it as: the more authority you have, the more Radical Candor costs you in political capital — and the more it is worth spending it
Key Questions
  • "Is there a conversation you have been softening — where you care so much about the person that you have stopped telling them the truth?"
  • "What would you say if you were certain they could handle it?"
  • "Where on this grid would the people who report to you place your most recent piece of feedback to them?"
  • "What is the cost of staying in Ruinous Empathy here — to them, to you, to the team?"
  • "What would it mean about you if this conversation went badly? That belief is the thing we need to look at."
Framework 05
Five Dysfunctions of a Team
Patrick Lencioni · The Five Dysfunctions of a Team (2002) · The Table Group · Five Behaviors facilitator certification via Wiley
Lencioni's model is built as a pyramid — each dysfunction sits on top of the last. You cannot solve Accountability if you do not have Commitment. You cannot have Commitment without Conflict. You cannot have Conflict without Trust. Start at the bottom.
Inattention to Results
The team prioritizes individual status, ego, or department goals over collective outcomes. You see this when teams report success while the organization is failing.
Avoidance of Accountability
Team members avoid holding peers accountable for low performance or behavior that violates standards. The leader becomes the only enforcer, which is unsustainable.
Lack of Commitment
Decisions are made but not truly owned. People leave the room saying yes and thinking no. Ambiguity is left unresolved because unresolved ambiguity feels safer than named disagreement.
Fear of Conflict
The team mistakes harmony for health. Difficult conversations happen outside the room, not in it. Ideas are not challenged. The "right" answer is suppressed in favor of keeping the peace.
Absence of Trust
Team members are not vulnerable with each other. Weaknesses, mistakes, and fears are hidden. This is not about personal trust — it is about the willingness to be seen clearly by peers.
Coaching Application — Individual Sessions
Use as a diagnostic: "Looking at this model, which level do you think your team is most stuck at?"
Trust dysfunction often shows up as: "I can't be honest with my team about what I don't know."
Conflict avoidance shows up as: "We all agreed in the meeting but nothing happened."
Accountability avoidance shows up as: "I am the only one who ever addresses underperformance."
Group Cohort Application — Session 3
Present the pyramid. Ask each cohort member: "Which level is your team's growth edge right now?"
Peer coaching triad: one person shares a real team situation, two others coach using the model as a diagnostic lens
Discussion anchor: "What would it mean to have productive conflict on your team? What would that look like differently from what happens now?"
Five Behaviors facilitator certification (Wiley) unlocks team-level assessment tools for organizational engagements
Key Questions
  • "When is the last time your team had a real conflict — not a fight, but an honest disagreement where people said what they actually thought?"
  • "If I sat in one of your team meetings, what would I not see that should be there?"
  • "Who on your team are you not holding accountable — and what is the story you tell yourself about why?"
  • "Does your team make decisions or make agreements? What is the difference in how they show up afterward?"
  • "What would trust look like on your team if it were fully present? What is missing right now?"
Framework 06
Everything DiSC
Behavioral framework based on William Moulton Marston's DISC theory (1928) · Everything DiSC is the Wiley-published, validated modern version · Certification required to administer profiles
DiSC describes how people tend to respond to their environment — specifically to challenges, people, pace, and rules. It is not a personality type. It is a behavioral pattern that shifts under pressure. Use it to help clients understand their default style and expand their range.
D · Dominance
Direct, decisive, driven
Motivated by results, competition, and winning. Fears losing control or being taken advantage of. Under pressure: becomes aggressive, impatient, or steamrolling. Coaching edge: slowing down to listen, considering impact on people, building buy-in before moving.
i · Influence
Enthusiastic, optimistic, collaborative
Motivated by enthusiasm, recognition, and relationships. Fears being rejected or ignored. Under pressure: becomes scattered, overcommits, avoids hard conversations. Coaching edge: following through, being direct when the relationship requires it, managing time.
S · Steadiness
Patient, collaborative, loyal
Motivated by stability, cooperation, and helping others. Fears conflict and sudden change. Under pressure: becomes conflict-avoidant, indecisive, or accommodating to the point of resentment. Coaching edge: asserting needs, tolerating ambiguity, leading change.
C · Conscientiousness
Analytical, accurate, systematic
Motivated by accuracy, quality, and expertise. Fears criticism and being wrong. Under pressure: becomes over-analytical, perfectionistic, or withdrawn. Coaching edge: making decisions with imperfect information, communicating confidence, releasing control.
Coaching Application
DiSC is most useful as a range expander, not a box. The question is never "you are a D, so you do this." It is: "your style pulls you toward X — when does that serve you, and when does it limit you?"
Under pressure, styles shift. A steady S can become either more accommodating or more stubborn. A dominant D can become more controlling. The coaching conversation lives in that pressure point.
Pair DiSC with 360 data: how does the client's self-reported style compare to how they are perceived under pressure?
For team coaching: map the team's styles visually. Where is there natural tension? Where is there a gap?
Common Client Patterns by Level
Managers: Often high-S or high-i — promoted for being collaborative, now struggling with direct feedback and holding boundaries
Directors: Often high-D or high-C — promoted for expertise or results, now needing to build influence rather than just deliver
Executives: Styles vary, but the pattern that shows up most is whatever style got them there is now limiting them at the next level
The most useful coaching question for any style: "What is the version of leadership you cannot access from this style alone?"
Key Questions
  • "Where on this grid does your style show up in how you lead? And where do you think you are perceived differently from how you intend to be?"
  • "Under pressure — when things are uncertain, when you feel criticized, when the stakes are high — which version of your style comes out?"
  • "What style is most represented on your team? What does that mean for how conflict shows up?"
  • "Which styles do you find easiest to work with? Which feel like friction? What does that friction actually cost you?"
Framework 07
CliftonStrengths — Coaching Application
Donald Clifton / Gallup · Originally published as StrengthsFinder 2.0 (2007) · gallup.com/cliftonstrengths · Certification optional but available
CliftonStrengths identifies 34 talent themes organized into four domains. Unlike assessments that describe personality, it describes where people have the most potential for excellence — their top themes represent natural patterns of thinking, feeling, and behavior that are already present and that coaching can amplify.
Executing
Themes about getting things done: Achiever, Arranger, Belief, Consistency, Deliberative, Discipline, Focus, Responsibility, Restorative. Clients with top themes here are most energized by action, completion, and order.
Influencing
Themes about leading others and making impact: Activator, Command, Communication, Competition, Maximizer, Self-Assurance, Significance, Woo. Clients with these themes are energized by persuading, speaking, and expanding reach.
Relationship Building
Themes about deep connection: Adaptability, Connectedness, Developer, Empathy, Harmony, Includer, Individualization, Positivity, Relator. These clients build loyalty and cohesion — and often struggle with authority and conflict.
Strategic Thinking
Themes about absorbing and analyzing information: Analytical, Context, Futuristic, Ideation, Input, Intellection, Learner, Strategic. These clients generate ideas and see patterns — and often struggle with follow-through and decisive action.
How to Use CliftonStrengths in Coaching
Debrief the themes in order of rank — not what they mean generically, but how they show up for this specific person in their specific context
Find the theme pairs that create tension — e.g., Activator + Deliberative, or Achiever + Relator. These internal tensions often explain leadership blocks
Name the shadow side — every theme has one. Responsibility can become martyrdom. Harmony can become conflict avoidance. Activator can become recklessness. The coaching work is often in the shadow.
Ask about energy, not just behavior — when do you feel most alive in your work? That is usually a top theme in full expression.
Common Coaching Patterns by Theme
Responsibility (top theme): Takes on too much. Coaching: what would it mean to let something fail that is not yours to carry?
Empathy (top theme): Absorbs others' emotions. Coaching: whose feelings are you carrying into this room that are not yours?
Command (top theme): Perceived as intimidating. Coaching: what would it cost you to stay quiet one beat longer?
Futuristic (top theme): Struggles to be present with what is. Coaching: what does this moment need from you before the future arrives?
Achiever (top theme): Rests with difficulty. Coaching: what would "enough" feel like, and is that fear or wisdom?
Key Questions for CliftonStrengths Debrief
  • "Looking at your top 5 — which one surprises you most? Which one did you see coming?"
  • "Tell me about a moment when your [top theme] was at full power. What did that look like? How did it feel?"
  • "Now tell me about a time when that same theme created problems for you or for others."
  • "If the people who work for you described your leadership in one sentence, which of these themes would they be describing?"
  • "Which themes are underused? Where in your work do you wish you could access more of that?"
  • "What theme do you wish you had that you do not? What is the cost of working around it?"
Framework 08
Immunity to Change
Robert Kegan and Lisa Laskow Lahey · Immunity to Change: How to Overcome It and Unlock the Potential in Yourself and Your Organization (2009) · Facilitator certification: Minds at Work (mindsatwork.com)
Immunity to Change is a framework for understanding why committed, motivated, intelligent people fail to change — even when they genuinely want to. The insight is that the barrier is not willpower or skill — it is a hidden competing commitment that the change would threaten. Every column of the ITC map reveals a deeper layer of the same structure.
The Four-Column Map
Column 1 Column 2 Column 3 Column 4
Improvement Goal
What do you want to change or improve?		 Doing/Not Doing Instead
What are you doing (or not doing) that works against that goal?		 Hidden Competing Commitment
If you imagine doing the opposite of Column 2 — what worry arises? What are you committed to preventing?		 Big Assumption
What must be true for Column 3 to be a real risk? What do you believe, deeply, about yourself or the world — that makes this feel necessary?
Example: "I want to be more direct with my team" → "I hold back, soften feedback, wait until something is really bad" → "I am committed to not being seen as harsh or threatening" → "I believe that if I am direct, people will think I am like the hard manager I had who hurt people, and that would mean I am not a good person."
Why This Matters in Coaching
Most coaching works at Column 1 (the goal) and Column 2 (the behavior). ITC goes deeper into what the behavior is actually protecting.
The Big Assumption in Column 4 is almost never rationally examined — it just operates. Surfacing it and testing it is where sustained change becomes possible.
Column 4 is not about childhood or therapy. It is about a belief about the world that was once adaptive and may now be limiting. That is a coaching conversation.
When to Use ITC in Your Practice
For clients who have been working on the same thing for a long time and keep coming up against the same wall
For Deep Dive clients in months 4–5, when surface-level goals have been addressed and the deeper work is available
Do not introduce ITC too early — the client needs to trust you and trust the process first
Facilitator certification (Minds at Work) is strongly recommended before using this as a formal tool — the map is simple; holding it well is not
Key Questions
  • "You have said you want this. And you are clearly capable. So what is in the way — not externally, but in you?"
  • "If you imagined doing the opposite of what you have been doing, what worry immediately shows up?"
  • "What are you protecting? What would be at risk if you changed?"
  • "What must be true about the world — or about you — for that risk to feel real?"
  • "Has there been a time when you tested that assumption and it did not hold? What happened?"
Framework 09
Conditions of Belonging + Equity Lens
Conditions of Belonging: National Equity Project · Equity Lab: national education sector · Your lived practice from Urban Teachers and national nonprofit DEI work
This is not an add-on to your coaching practice — it is the foundation of it. Your equity lens is a differentiator because it comes from having led organizational systems, not from a training. Use it where others cannot: in coaching senior leaders who need to examine what their leadership signals, permits, and perpetuates.
Conditions of Belonging — The Core Framework
Valued
Do people feel their contributions are recognized and that they matter to the collective work? Who in this organization is seen, and who is invisible?
Connected
Do people have authentic relationships across difference? Or are connections clustered by identity, role, or proximity to power?
Contributing
Do people have meaningful agency in decisions that affect them? Who gets to influence how things work here, and who is consulted only after the fact?
Coaching Application — Individual Sessions
Use as a diagnostic for leaders who are building or inheriting teams. "Walk me through each of these three conditions in your team right now. Who has full belonging? Who does not?"
Connect to leadership shadow: "What does your leadership signal to someone who does not look like you or did not come up the way you did?"
For leaders who say they "don't see race" or "treat everyone the same" — use Conditions of Belonging as a data-based alternative: what does the data say about who thrives here, who advances, who stays?
Equity Lens in Executive Coaching
Not every client is ready for equity as a named frame. The coaching move is to get there through data and questions, not position.
Use Ripple Effect Mapping to help leaders trace the downstream impact of their decisions across different populations
The most powerful equity question in executive coaching: "Who benefits from how things work here — and who does not? And how much of that is by design?"
Frame equity leadership as what it actually is: more sophisticated leadership, not moral positioning
Key Questions
  • "If I asked the people on the margins of your team to describe what it feels like to work here — what would they say that the people in the center would not?"
  • "What patterns show up in who advances, who speaks in meetings, who is given the benefit of the doubt? What do those patterns tell you?"
  • "What does your leadership permission? And what does it prohibit — even unintentionally?"
  • "Who has to work hardest to be seen as competent in your organization? What would change if that were different?"
  • "What would it cost you — politically, relationally, practically — to name this publicly? And what is the cost of not naming it?"
Framework 10
ICF Core Competencies
International Coaching Federation · Updated 2019 · 8 competencies across 4 domains · Foundation for ACC, PCC, and MCC credentialing · coachingfederation.org
The ICF Core Competencies are not a checklist — they are a description of what excellent coaching looks like in practice. Know them well enough that you do not think about them in session. Your aim is to embody them, not perform them.
Domain A · Foundation
1. Demonstrates Ethical Practice — honesty, integrity, confidentiality, managing conflicts of interest. Know the ICF Code of Ethics before your first paid session.
2. Embodies a Coaching Mindset — curiosity over certainty. Not trying to fix. Trusting that the client has the resources and the answers. This is the hardest competency for experienced leaders to embody — you are used to having answers.
Domain B · Co-Creating the Relationship
3. Establishes and Maintains Agreements — clear contracts per session and per engagement. What are we working on? How will we know it worked?
4. Cultivates Trust and Safety — the coaching relationship holds the coaching work. Without safety, clients perform rather than explore. This is built, not assumed.
5. Maintains Presence — full attention. No fixing, no advice-giving, no half-listening while composing your next question. Presence is the most frequently cited differentiator between good and excellent coaches.
Domain C · Communicating Effectively
6. Listens Actively — what is said, how it is said, what is not said, and what the body is communicating. Active listening is not just being quiet. It is absorbing and reflecting the whole person.
7. Evokes Awareness — asking questions that expand rather than confirm. Sharing observations. Challenging with care. This is where coaching produces insight that the client could not have reached alone.
Domain D · Cultivating Learning and Growth
8. Facilitates Client Growth — translating insight to action. Supporting integration. Partnering on what comes next. The session ends; the work continues. The coach's job is to make that continuation more likely.
Which Competencies Are Most Assessed at ACC
The ICF assesses recorded sessions. The competencies most frequently cited in feedback: Maintains Presence (5) — are you truly present or processing? Listens Actively (6) — are you listening to the whole person or just the words? Evokes Awareness (7) — are you asking questions that open new territory or confirming what you already think?
The Competency That Trips Up Experienced Leaders
Embodies a Coaching Mindset (2) is consistently the hardest for high-achieving leaders entering coaching. The belief that you should add value, share your perspective, and leverage your expertise is the exact thing you have to set aside in the coaching room. Your experience makes you want to solve. The coaching mindset requires you to ask instead.
